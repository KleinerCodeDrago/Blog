My Experience with E-Book Readers: Kindle Oasis and Books Tab X

A review of the Kindle Oasis and Books Tab X e-book readers.

Introduction#

As a long-time user of e-readers, I’ve spent several years with the Kindle Oasis. Recently, I’ve ventured into the world of Android-based e-readers with the Books Tab X, and I’m here to share my thoughts. There’s a lot to love—and a few frustrations—about both devices, especially when it comes to format support, portability, and versatility.

The Kindle Oasis: A Premium Reading Experience with Frustrating Limitations#

I own a Kindle Oasis, likely the third generation, which was released in 2019. This device offers several premium features, such as:

Pros:#

  • 7-inch E Ink Display with 300 ppi Resolution: The high resolution and sharp display make reading a pleasure, whether it’s manga or text-heavy e-books
  • 25-LED Adjustable Warm Light: This feature is perfect for night reading, allowing me to adjust both the brightness and warmth of the light for a more comfortable experience
  • Physical Page-Turn Buttons: These buttons are a lifesaver for one-handed reading, especially when I’m too tired to tap the screen

However, despite its high-quality design, the Oasis comes with a few major downsides:

Cons:#

  • Limited Format Support: Historically, Kindle has never supported EPUB. Amazon stuck to MOBI for a long time and later switched to its proprietary AZW3 (Kindle Format 8) This change has made it harder to transfer non-Amazon e-books onto the Kindle without converting them via Calibre or using the Send-to-Kindle service.
  • Browser and App Issues: The Kindle Oasis’s browser is virtually unusable. It’s slow and struggles with even the most basic websites. Worse still, it can’t support apps like Libby or Onleihe, which makes borrowing e-books from libraries impossible

Despite these shortcomings, I still love the Kindle Oasis for its form factor and reading comfort. But as someone who values owning e-books outright and using a variety of formats, the limitations really stand out.

The Books Tab X: Versatility at the Cost of Portability#

Recently, I purchased the Books Tab X, a massive 13.3-inch Android-based E-Ink tablet. While it’s not as portable as my Kindle, it offers a level of versatility that Kindle simply can’t match.

Pros:#

  • App Support: Being an Android-based device, the Tab X allows me to install apps like Libby and Onleihe, which makes borrowing e-books much easier. I no longer need to convert formats, and I can even use RSS readers like Pocket to keep up with articles.
  • Larger Display: For reading manga and other visually dense content, the 13.3-inch screen is a game-changer. The larger size makes it easy to read without constantly zooming in, which was a problem on the smaller Kindle Oasis.

Cons:#

  • Size and Weight: The Books Tab X is huge compared to the Kindle, and I’m still getting used to carrying it around. It’s not as convenient for casual reading sessions during meals or while lounging.

Although it’s not as compact, I can see myself using the Books Tab X for longer reading sessions at home, where I can appreciate the large screen. The ability to access apps and read a wider variety of formats makes this device ideal for someone like me, who values flexibility over portability.

Conclusion: Kindle Oasis vs. Books Tab X#

After using both devices, here’s my take: the Kindle Oasis is perfect for readers who are deeply entrenched in Amazon’s ecosystem. Its premium features and ergonomic design make it a great choice for casual, everyday reading—especially if you mainly purchase books from Amazon. However, its lack of support for common formats like EPUB and its inability to handle borrowed e-books natively really limit its appeal.

On the other hand, the Books Tab X offers a more versatile reading experience, especially for those who use library services or need more control over what they read. But the large size means it’s not the most portable option, so it’s better suited for home use rather than on-the-go reading.

If you’re deciding between the two, the choice ultimately comes down to your reading habits. If you’re locked into the Amazon ecosystem and want something lightweight, the Kindle Oasis might still be your best bet. But if you want to read anything and everything, especially borrowed books and articles, the Books Tab X is the clear winner.

I’ll be experimenting more with the Tab X in the coming weeks and might write a follow-up post about how well I’ve adjusted to it.
